Economics Research

Algorithmic Pricing and Personalized Price Discrimination

This research explored why online platforms may present different prices to different users and how consumer psychology can influence pricing decisions.

Research Question

I noticed that the same product, hotel, or ticket could have different prices across users and platforms. I therefore examined how browsing behavior, order history, regular spending levels, time pressure, and consumer preferences could be related to personalized pricing.

Data Collection Compared the same products across multiple platforms and collected information from different users.
Variables Considered browsing frequency, ordering frequency, regular spending level, and purchase timing.
Analysis Used statistical and economic reasoning to examine pricing patterns and consumer behavior.

Main Observations

  • Platforms can use consumer behavior data to create digital user profiles.
  • Anchoring and the decoy effect may influence how consumers judge whether a price is reasonable.
  • Time pressure and the effort required to compare platforms may cause consumers to accept higher prices.
  • Consumer preferences and repeated purchasing habits can reduce willingness to switch to alternatives.

Fairness and Efficiency

Personalized pricing may improve market efficiency or make some products accessible to different groups. However, it can also create fairness concerns when consumers cannot understand how prices are determined or when loyal users are charged more.

Possible Responses

  • Improve pricing transparency and access to comparison tools.
  • Strengthen oversight of extreme or discriminatory price changes.
  • Provide consumers with multiple purchasing options.
  • Encourage consumers to compare prices and protect their rights.
Student-Led Project

Magic Box

An anonymous voice platform created to support student emotional well-being and provide a safer space for honest expression.

Pilot Across Three Grade Levels

Anonymous Voice Platform for Youth

Originated from a Social Emotional Learning course

During a Social Emotional Learning course, my team and I focused on mental health and well-being. We noticed that high school students were facing increasing pressure but often lacked a safe place to talk about their experiences. I recruited team members and initiated Magic Box as an anonymous channel where students could write about thoughts they did not feel comfortable sharing with friends or family.

1. Research Collected information about youth mental health and considered potential challenges with teachers.
2. Promotion Created collection boxes and promotional materials and introduced the project throughout the international division.
3. Collection Collected anonymous messages and encouraged participation with positive notes and small gifts.
4. Response Organized messages by themes, created a presentation and video, and held classroom discussions.

Message Categories

  • Family relationships
  • Friendship
  • Romantic relationships
  • Academic pressure

Purpose

  • Create a judgment-free channel for self-expression.
  • Help students feel heard, understood, and supported.
  • Build empathy through shared experiences.
  • Encourage a more positive and supportive school environment.
The project ran across all three high school grade levels. A future online expansion through Root & Shoot has been considered but is currently paused.

Volunteer Work in Qingdao

Interaction with children with autism spectrum disorder

I regularly visited a center for autistic children in Qingdao. I tried different ways to interact with the children, including games, chess, and calligraphy. I found that some children had difficulty following instructions or maintaining attention for long periods.

During one music class, I noticed that classical music gradually helped a restless classroom become quieter. Some children began moving their hands with the rhythm.

Discovering the Handpan

Building trust through sound and free exploration

I introduced a handpan, whose shape led the children to call it a “UFO.” After playing a short melody, I allowed them to explore the instrument freely and create their own rhythms without strict instructions.

This experience helped me build trust with the children and inspired me to think about how classical music and handpan activities could support emotional recognition and self-expression.

This experience became the starting point for the Harmony Bridge concept, which is currently in the planning stage and temporarily paused.
